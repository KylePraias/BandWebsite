History

Green Day emerged from the vibrant punk scene of California's East Bay in 1987, initially performing under the name Sweet Children. The band quickly became a fixture at Berkeley's 924 Gilman Street club, a hub for punk enthusiasts. Their early releases, including the EP 1,000 Hours and the album 39/Smooth, were launched under the independent label Lookout! Records, helping them build a dedicated local following. In 1994, Green Day's major-label debut, Dookie, propelled them to international fame, selling over 20 million copies worldwide. The album's success, driven by singles like "Basket Case" and "When I Come Around," played a pivotal role in bringing punk rock into the mainstream. Subsequent albums such as Insomniac (1995), Nimrod (1997), and Warning (2000) showcased the band's evolving sound and willingness to experiment beyond their punk roots. The 2004 release of American Idiot, a politically charged rock opera, marked a significant resurgence for Green Day, resonating with a new generation and earning critical acclaim. The album's narrative and themes were later adapted into a successful Broadway musical. Continuing their momentum, the band released 21st Century Breakdown in 2009, followed by the ambitious trilogy ¡Uno!, ¡Dos!, and ¡Tré! in 2012. Green Day's influence and longevity were recognized with their induction into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 2015. They have sold over 85 million records globally, underscoring their enduring impact on the music industry. In recent years, the band has continued to produce new material, including the albums Revolution Radio (2016) and Father of All ... (2020), demonstrating their commitment to evolving their sound while staying true to their punk origins.
